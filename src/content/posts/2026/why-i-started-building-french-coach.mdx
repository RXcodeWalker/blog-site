# Why I Started Building French Coach

When I was five years old, my parents signed me up for French classes once or twice a week. It was just a fun little after-school thing, but honestly, it didn't prepare me at all for what happened next.

In first and second grade, when I was around seven and eight, I moved to a bilingual school. Half our day was in English, and the other half was entirely in French. Suddenly, I was trying to learn actual subjects like math and science in a language I barely even knew!

It was terrifying. When I walked into that first-grade classroom, it felt like everyone else was miles ahead of me, especially when it came to speaking. They were chatting almost effortlessly, while I could barely string a few words together. But I kept at it, listened closely, and eventually caught up. Because we were using the language constantly to talk and think, pronunciation just became second nature to me. Instead of memorizing boring vocabulary lists, I was actually using French to solve math problems and talk to my friends. It got to the point where, after just one year, my French teacher would stop and tell me that I had the accent of a native speaker. You can imagine how thrilled my second-grade self was!

Honestly, I didn’t think much of it at the time. It was just my normal. But then, in Grade 3, everything changed. I moved to a standard English-medium school, and suddenly, French was shoved into a completely different box. It became just another subject on a timetable.

The shift was jarring. The vibrant, living language I grew up speaking was replaced by endless grammar grids, reading comprehension worksheets, and writing drills. We spent hours dissecting text, but almost zero time actually talking.

For years, I just accepted that this was how language classes worked. But fast forward to tenth grade, and that was when the reality of the situation really hit me.

I looked around at my classmates. These were incredibly smart kids, many of whom had been studying French for years. Yet, the moment the teacher asked a question that required an on-the-spot spoken answer? Pure panic. The room would go dead silent.

That was the turning point for me. It all became so obvious.

> **Students don't struggle because they don't know French. They struggle because they never get to actually speak it.**

Think about how a normal classroom works. You’ve got twenty-five, maybe thirty students, and one teacher. Classes happen maybe twice a week. If you map out the math, each student gets what, maybe sixty seconds of actual speaking time per class?

It's impossible for a teacher, no matter how amazing they are, to give every single person personalized, detailed feedback on their accent or sentence structure.

The result? Speaking becomes terrifying. Students get trapped in this slow, agonizing cycle of translating English to French in their heads, getting scared of making a pronunciation mistake, and ultimately just staying quiet.

Between eighth and ninth grade, I had an experience that changed my entire outlook on why we learn languages in the first place. I traveled to France during my summer vacation.

Once we got outside the typical tourist bubbles, I quickly realized that many local people didn't speak much English at all. If I wanted to get food or navigate around, I had to use what I knew. So, I started talking. I ordered meals at small bistros, asked for directions on the street, and chatted with locals completely in French, without ever pulling out Google Translate.

The coolest part was how people reacted. Several locals were genuinely surprised and impressed by my French, especially because they weren't expecting it from someone my age coming from abroad.

That trip was the ultimate confirmation for me: language doesn't exist to pass exams or fill out worksheets. It exists to connect with people.

Yet, even as someone who loved the language and went on to score a 97% (A\*) in my Cambridge IGCSEs and earn distinctions in the DELF A2 and B1 exams, I still felt the gap when I got back to school. Now that I've started preparing for my IBDP French B oral exams and looking ahead to the DELF B2, I found myself wishing for a better tool to keep those real-world speaking skills sharp.

So, I tried the popular language apps. You know the ones I mean, like Duolingo and Babbel.

They are honestly great for building a daily habit, learning vocabulary, and keeping you motivated.

But when it came to actual speaking, they left me hanging.

They’re fine for quick quizzes or repeating pre-recorded phrases, but they don’t feel like talking to a human being. They tell you if you're "Correct" or "Incorrect," but they never answer the questions you actually have.

Even the newer AI conversation apps I tried fell short. While they were good for casual chatting, they didn't offer any help with the actual frameworks you need for intense oral exams like the IGCSE or DELF boards.

That is the exact seed that started French Coach.

I didn't want to build another standard language app or a generic AI chatbot that just spits out grammatically flawless text. We have enough of those! I wanted to build something that felt like practicing with a real, patient teacher who is available in your pocket twenty-four hours a day.

What's cool about French Coach isn't just the fact that it talks back to you. The main thing is that it actually remembers you.

Most language tools treat every single conversation like you're a total stranger. You open the app, do an exercise, close it, and the app forgets you exist.

French Coach is designed to remember you over time, just like a real tutor would. It tracks your recurring pronunciation habits, notices the specific grammar slips you repeat when you're tired, and figures out your vocabulary gaps.

A few years ago, you couldn't really build something like this. The tech just wasn't there to reliably listen to a student's accent, analyze a spoken sentence on the fly, and keep a long-term memory of how that student is actually progressing.

The moment I realized this was actually possible, I couldn't stop thinking about it.

This is how what started as a simple idea has eventually turned into one of the biggest software projects I've ever worked on. Trying to build things like speech recognition, real-time pronunciation feedback, an AI memory system, and adaptive coaching has been way harder than I ever expected. I've spent hours debugging things that drove me crazy, but that's also what has made the whole process so rewarding.

Instead of an app blindly marking an answer wrong and moving on, I wanted French Coach to look at your language habits over time.

It shouldn't just say:

> "Wrong tense."

It should say something like:

> "Hey, your use of the subjunctive is getting much more natural, but you're still dropping the pronunciation liaison when you say _rendez-vous_ or _les amis_. Let's practice three sentences to lock that in."

I'm focusing heavily on nailing just a couple of core things.

First is natural conversation practice, where the app tailors scenarios around your specific skill level and exam goals, whether you are prepping for a formal DELF presentation or just trying to survive a roleplay where you order food under pressure.

Second is the memory part. Every practice session builds on the last one. If you had a rough time with past-tense verb agreements on Tuesday, the system remembers that blind spot and subtly brings it back up during your review sessions later in the week.

But look, I’m not building this for fluent speakers, and honestly, I’m not even building it just to help students chase a perfect test score.

Even though I’ve had to navigate the exam systems myself, I’ve always believed that education has to be about more than just scoring marks. Exams are just milestones along the way.

The real goal?

I'm building this for the version of me who wanted more opportunities to speak without feeling judged.

I’m building it for the student who only has French class twice a week, who doesn't have a native speaker at home to practice with, or who can’t afford an expensive private tutor.

I want to give people the confidence to speak so naturally that the language becomes a part of who they are, not just a grade on a transcript.

I'm building French Coach because speaking is the hardest part of learning French, yet it's the part students get to practice the least.

What helped me become comfortable speaking French all those years ago wasn't memorizing more words on a page. It was the fact that I was forced to use the language to communicate, to make mistakes, and to talk to real people.

French Coach is my attempt to give that exact same experience to every single learner, anywhere in the world.

At the end of the day, language isn't a puzzle to solve on paper.

It's a bridge to connect with people.

And to get across that bridge, you just have to start talking.
